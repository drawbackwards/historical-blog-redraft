Back-end development directly shapes the user experience your product delivers. Performance, reliability, and security are not back-end concerns that live in isolation from UX -- they are UX. If the back end is slow, broken, or insecure, no amount of beautiful front-end design will save you.

By Ward Andrews

## How Does Back-End Development Affect User Experience?

Most people think UX is about front-end design. It is not -- or at least, not entirely. UX encompasses everything a user sees and feels while interacting with your product. That includes how the product actually performs, not just how it is designed to look.

Climbing the Experience Success Ladder means delivering an experience that is high-performing, reliable, and secure. That requires building a back end that supports your designs and can adapt to the changing needs of your users. It also requires great communication from the first sketches to the final code review.

In a world where new features and technologies pop up daily, staying focused on the quality of your core product -- and the environment that supports it -- is what separates good products from great ones.

Here are four ways back-end development can help you build and maintain the kind of meaningful user experience that leads to lasting success.

## Does Back-End Performance Really Drive UX?

Yes. How your product performs day to day is the essence of your user experience.

When users spend an inordinate amount of time dealing with bugs, they start to form the wrong mental model of your product. They think it is broken. They think it does not work the way they expected it to.

Where do we see performance lag the most? Usually in the one place where you need to make a strong first impression -- first page load times. Users expect your site or application to load within two seconds. At three seconds or more, abandonment rates shoot through the roof.

Nobody sets out to build heavy pages with slow load times. Software products and websites grow over time. Charts, videos, photo galleries, plug-ins, and widgets creep in slowly and lead to death by a thousand cuts. One day you wake up and realise your product is moving like a three-toed sloth.

### How Do You Optimise Performance to Boost UX?

There is no one-size-fits-all approach, but there are a couple of fundamentals to keep in mind. Work closely with your full team of designers, developers, product managers, and business owners to find the solutions that are right for your product.

**Consider progressive page loading based on user actions**

If most of your users do not immediately scroll below the fold, why worry about delivering every piece of content right when the page loads? It is a big drag on user experience to make everybody wait for content that only some people want.

Apps like Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter have changed user expectations with seemingly infinite scrolling experiences. We are now accustomed to scrolling at our own pace and letting the system catch up with us.

Think about less popular actions you can move to the background while users explore their first-priority content. By coordinating your front-end design with back-end delivery, you can set the right expectations for your users while still delivering a seamless experience.

**Optimise API calls and data structures for better response times**

This requires that you understand the problem your users are actually trying to solve. Once you see the problem from their perspective, you can better decide how to efficiently access and deliver the data they need without overtaxing your servers.

Look for ways to trim data that is not needed and save it for another API call -- or remove it altogether. Batch the most-used data together and cache it on local systems as much as possible. Save less important data for specific user-generated requests.

Every little bit counts when making calls to the server. We have seen time and again in user testing that the speed of data retrieval has a bigger impact on user experience than how pretty it looks when it finally arrives.

## Can Reliability Issues Kill Your UX?

Yes, and quickly. Reliability is a baseline expectation. It will not earn your product a lot of bonus points, but it can certainly kill your reputation if it is bad.

It is tempting to think of reliability as black and white -- either something works or it does not. But reliability is relative. If things fail often enough, users will assume your product does not work and there is no hope. They will give up on it.

Users want to know that your app, service, or website will be available when they need it. Nothing reflects worse on your company than the feeling that your users and customers cannot rely on you. This is as basic and simple as uptime. How consistently are you up and running without major issues or problems?

This is not about being perfect. Software breaks down. It is bound to happen. Things go wrong and your users understand that. But you need to do everything in your power to make downtime as painless as possible.

Users are evaluating you based on how you respond to challenges. They want to see that you are responsive and they want to feel that you have everything under control. How you communicate and handle problems speaks volumes about your capacity to serve your users and customers over the long haul.

Reliability issues usually come from a flaw in the underlying technology and infrastructure, or in your own code. Either way, it is important to address even the smallest problems that can chip away at your reliability.

People feel anxious about using unreliable products, whether they admit it or not. Like any tool, they want to know your product will work and they will not be able to break it. That is what ultimately motivates them to keep coming back.

### How Do You Build Reliability and Preserve UX?

If you have a product, expect it to go down at some point. It is really a question of "when," not "if." You can manage this by building consistency in some core areas.

**Provide clear and consistent communication to users**

Communication will not fix all your problems, but it will make them easier to handle. You can plan for system-wide issues ahead of time. You can craft effective error messages and set them up to appear based on specific technical triggers. These messages help reassure users that the problem is with you, not them -- and that you are working on it.

Communication can also guide users to their next steps. Telling them when you expect to be back up and running, and what other solutions you can offer, can prevent a short-term problem from building into a long-term issue.

**Build availability trackers for critical functions**

Some products are so critical that users' jobs or lives may depend on them. Utilities or business-centric applications need to be available 24/7 to keep processes running. These situations call for an availability tracker that shows in real time how the system is performing and reassures users that all is well.

A tracker like this can also be crucial to determining whether a problem is local and temporary or system-wide and long-term. Users need a central source of truth that will tell them if the system is down so they can find another way to work around the problem. The last thing you want when something goes wrong is to keep your users guessing.

**Synchronise across devices**

We are no longer in a world where you can assume your users will only access your product through a single device. You need to deliver a consistent and reliable experience across a variety of devices. Automatic data synchronisation is a baseline expectation for most users. Forcing them to sync their own data -- or not offering that at all -- can tank your user experience.

## Are Great UX and Security Mutually Exclusive?

No. Security is often seen as a direct trade-off with user experience. In general, the most secure applications in the world tend to have terrible user experiences, and the best UX ideas are often considered big security risks.

The reality is that most products exist somewhere in the middle. It is about finding the right balance between managing security risks and getting the most ROI possible out of your UX.

The good news is that it does not have to be a question of choosing between two extremes. There is almost always a way to creatively improve UX and stay within specific security constraints.

The best example of this is the dreaded password. We are all familiar with the process of creating and recovering passwords, and we have all had some terrible experiences. It is true that nobody wants their account hacked, so it is not something you can ignore. But there is a big difference between your Reddit account and your bank account.

You can optimise user experience by easing up on some of the password requirements that do not actually increase security. The friction you take out of the process will help the general perception of your product and increase security for your users.

### How Do You Increase Security While Improving UX?

Like in any industry, people in product development copy each other without questioning why. Here are some ways we have tried to break the mould with our clients to help improve their products while keeping them secure.

**Build a culture of security**

Too many product teams and companies think of security as a legal question. This breeds a culture of gatekeepers who will answer questions on the basis of one set of security-focused criteria. They will often lock things down even more than necessary out of a reasonable concern for caution. But if everybody from design to development is aware and educated on security principles, you can get a lot more creative in your solutions.

**Make security a default setting**

You do not want to force users to remember to install security updates or go in search of the newest update to your product. This not only puts a huge burden on them -- impacting user experience -- but it leads to a lot of holes in your security. Make it easy for users to feel and stay secure in the environment you are building for them. That security will improve confidence, which is the lifeblood of good user experience.

## What About Everything Else That Affects UX?

No matter what, invisible technical dependencies on the back end will always impact visible user experience on the front end. From automatically generated error messages that seem written by a robot, to form fields that do not work right, you cannot anticipate every problem.

Take a super common feature like exporting a dataset as an Excel spreadsheet or in simple CSV format. This seems straightforward, but what if it is a particularly large dataset? That can increase the time it takes to process the request. What if your user has a terrible internet connection? Nothing you do to speed up the export can make up for that.

You could create messaging that estimates the amount of time the download will take and shows a loading page. That is a good front-end solution. But it could create a heavy load on backend API calls, or the user could have a bad internet connection.

A collaborative solution might acknowledge the request and then send an email when the download is ready. This kind of elegant workaround requires more than a front-end design or a back-end solution. It requires collaboration and careful planning of resources.

### How Should Back-End and Front-End Teams Work Together?

It is important that back-end developers work hand-in-hand with front-end designers on a regular basis. They need to understand the technical specs as well as the intention behind the design. That will help them make the right decisions to contribute to the success of the product.

Likewise, front-end designers need to understand the technical limitations of the product. The two need to be in constant conversation to find the best solution that solves several problems at the same time.
