# Problem Framing: Gain a New Perspective

**The key to a successful solution is a well-framed problem.** Even a slight shift in how you look at a problem can lead to a completely different outcome. The Powers of Ten technique is one practical way to break out of linear thinking and find the right level at which to frame a problem.

This is Part 2 in our series sharing tried and true methods for helping teams frame problems. [Read Part 1.]

## What Is the Problem Framing Challenge Teams Face?

Teams solving product and UX problems often end up building solutions to problems that don't actually exist. They get heads-down, fixate on one or two strategies, and lose sight of the bigger picture. The result is a well-executed answer to the wrong question.

A small adjustment in how you look at a problem can change everything.

## What Is the Powers of Ten Technique?

The Powers of Ten technique is a reframing exercise that asks teams to explore a problem at different levels of scale. It's based on a short film made by Charles and Ray Eames in 1977. The film opens on a picnic in Chicago from one meter away, zooms out to the edge of the known universe, then zooms back in to the level of a single atom.

The technique works because it forces you off the single vantage point where you're stuck and makes you consider what the problem looks like at very different magnitudes.

## How Does the Powers of Ten Exercise Work?

The exercise is straightforward to run, but there's a lot behind it.

1. **Choose a scale** that fits your problem. This could be distance, time, size, cost, complexity, or something more abstract.
2. **Explore different levels of magnitude** along that scale. Zoom in. Zoom out. Go further in both directions than feels comfortable.
3. **Notice patterns** that repeat at different levels of the scale.
4. **Identify the level** at which you think your team can make the biggest difference.

As you move through the levels, ideas that usually sit in isolation start to collide in new ways. That's the point.

## Why Do Our Best Ideas Come in the Shower?

Those random flashes of insight in the shower, on a run, or driving a long stretch of highway aren't actually random. They're the product of a specific process in the brain.

When we're doing routine, comforting activities, our brains get flooded with dopamine. That dopamine flood pulls us out of our linear thinking process and allows ideas that normally stay isolated to randomly collide in new ways. The mundane unlocks the creative.

The Powers of Ten technique is designed to trigger that same free-association mindset on purpose, without having to wait for a shower thought to strike.

## Why Can't You Design a Solution in Isolation?

Because every problem is part of a larger system.

The issues you're solving in product design don't exist in a vacuum. They're connected to both smaller, more granular details and larger contextual forces. A solution that only accounts for one level of the problem is a fragile solution.

The Powers of Ten technique helps your team hold both the granular and the broad context in mind at the same time, so the solution you build actually fits within the system it lives in.

## What the Exercise Is Not For

The goal is not to get frozen by layers of complexity. The point isn't to solve every level of the scale at once.

Use the technique to identify where you can make the biggest difference, then frame your problem at that level. It's a tool for getting unstuck and generating new directions, not a reason to expand scope indefinitely.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is problem framing in UX design?**
Problem framing is the process of clearly defining and positioning a problem before attempting to solve it. A well-framed problem points your team toward the right solution space. A poorly framed one can lead to building the right thing for the wrong problem.

**What is the Powers of Ten technique and how is it used in UX?**
It's a perspective-shifting exercise based on the 1977 Eames film. Teams choose a scale relevant to their problem, then explore it at different levels of magnitude, zooming in and out until patterns emerge and the right framing level becomes clear.

**Why do teams end up solving the wrong problem?**
Usually because they start designing before they've properly defined the problem. Teams get close to the work, fixate on familiar approaches, and lose perspective on whether the problem they're solving is the actual problem worth solving.

**How does free association help with creative problem solving?**
Free association lets ideas that are usually isolated from each other collide in unexpected ways. It breaks linear thinking. Techniques like Powers of Ten are designed to deliberately trigger that state so you don't have to wait for a shower thought to do it for you.

**When should a team use the Powers of Ten technique?**
Use it when your team feels stuck on a problem, when you suspect you might be solving at the wrong level, or when you want to pressure-test your current problem framing before committing to a solution direction.
