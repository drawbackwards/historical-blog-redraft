*This is one of a series of blog posts exploring what product thinking is, why it matters, and how to develop it in your organization.*

Product thinking is the practice of clearly identifying the core problem your users need to solve before deciding how to solve it. It keeps your product useful by bridging the gap between its purpose (what makes it useful) and its functionality (how it's useful). For UX design teams especially, it should be central to everything.

Once you know the problem, the next step is digging into the specific jobs your customers are hiring your product to do. That's where product thinking gets really powerful.

## What Is Product Thinking?

Product thinking is a way of approaching design and development that starts with the problem, not the solution. Rather than jumping straight to features and functionality, it asks: what real problem are we solving, for whom, and why does it matter?

It's the difference between building something and building the right something.

### Why Does Product Thinking Matter for UX Teams?

Without product thinking, it's easy to end up with a product full of features that nobody needed. Product thinking keeps the team anchored to the user's real problem, which is what separates useful products from bloated ones.

It also gives everyone on the team a shared filter for evaluating new ideas and requests. That matters more than most people realise.

## What Does "Jobs to Be Done" Mean in Product Thinking?

Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) is a framework that asks why customers are really using your product. The idea is that users "hire" a product to help them get past a specific challenge and reach a better state on the other side.

It's not just about the task they're completing. It's about the underlying motivation driving them to complete it. Why do they want to get that job done in the first place?

The more clearly you can define that job, the better you'll address the real "why" behind your product.

## How Do You Start With the "Why"?

**Start with your product's vision.** Every product needs one. It's why your product exists, why your team exists, and why you're doing what you're doing.

Identifying the problems your product addresses tells you what it exists to do. But why it does that is a different question altogether.

Good product thinking articulates the reasons behind your product:

- Why is now the right time to build this?
- Why do customers need your product?
- Why are they motivated to choose it over other solutions?

Having a clear vision of that "why" makes it much easier to define the specific jobs you want your customer to hire the product to do.

## How Do You Identify the Jobs Your Product Needs to Do?

Once you're clear on the why, the next step is figuring out how you're going to help get the job done.

What makes products meaningful is when they provide a solution that perfectly fits a problem. Your ability to fit the solution to the problem is what will determine the user's overall experience.

Good product thinking identifies:

- **What** problem you're solving
- **For whom** you're solving it
- **Why** you're solving it
- **How** you'll help users accomplish their specific jobs and goals

This is where the rubber meets the road. You have to define the features that will have the most impact. It's hard enough to design good features and visuals, but it's even harder to design the right thing for the right job.

## How Does Product Thinking Help Teams Avoid Feature Bloat?

Product thinking empowers everyone in your organisation to question new feature requests, not just the product manager.

When you develop a culture that values product thinking, the whole team can ask:

- Is this the right tool for the job?
- Does it fit into the product?
- Does it solve a real problem?

If the answer is no, or you're not sure, it's time to go back and ask more questions before bloating your product with features that may or may not help get the job done.

Product thinking enables you to ask the right questions, build the right features, and more efficiently steer away from random one-off requests that don't contribute to the larger problem you're trying to solve.

As difficult as that can be, it's also genuinely empowering.

## FAQ

**What is product thinking in UX design?**
Product thinking is the practice of clearly defining the core problem your users need to solve before designing any solution. It ensures your product stays useful by keeping design decisions grounded in real user needs rather than assumptions.

**What is the Jobs-to-be-Done framework?**
Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) is a framework that treats customers as people who "hire" a product to help them overcome a specific challenge and reach a better state. It focuses on the underlying motivation behind a task, not just the task itself.

**Why should the whole team practice product thinking, not just the product manager?**
Because feature decisions happen at every level of a team. When everyone understands what job the product is supposed to do, everyone can evaluate new requests against that standard. That shared filter is what prevents feature bloat and keeps the product focused.

**How does product thinking prevent feature bloat?**
By giving the team a clear question to ask about every new idea: does this help solve the core problem? If it doesn't, or nobody is sure, that's a signal to stop and ask more questions before building anything.

**Where do I start if I want to apply product thinking to my product?**
Start with the "why." Get clear on your product vision, define the real problem your users face, and then use a Jobs-to-be-Done approach to understand the deeper motivation driving your users. From there, you can define features that actually fit the job.
