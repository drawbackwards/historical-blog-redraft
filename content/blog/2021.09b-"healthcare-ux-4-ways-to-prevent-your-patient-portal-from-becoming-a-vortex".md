# Healthcare UX: 4 Ways to Prevent Your Patient Portal from Becoming a Vortex

The hard truth is that most healthcare patient portals are not built for patients first. They're built for the cost-saving concerns of the business. We covered the four problems that most healthcare patient portals face in our last post. Now it's time for some solutions.

We've been working on healthcare websites, mobile apps and portals for more than 20 years and we've seen a lot of things go wrong. Here are four key ways you can prevent your patients from getting lost in the vortex of a poorly designed and executed portal.

## What's the Best Way to Improve a Healthcare Patient Portal?

Start by actually getting to know your users. The best way to build a useful and effective portal is to put in the time and effort upfront to understand the real-world needs of each of your users. Most healthcare providers boil down their users to a few general user types -- patients, physicians and employers or brokers -- and don't dig any deeper than that.

But what about people who have accessibility issues? What about the health needs and technology expectations of different age groups? How does the user interface translate when a physician or their staff is moving from a tablet to a desktop and back again?

To encourage adoption and use, you need to know how to accommodate the needs of administrative staff looking for insurance information, patients looking for doctors, and physicians looking for in-depth health history. Then you need to find the edge use cases and build solutions to address those on the margins.

Taking the time to understand who you're supporting with the portal and how you can best support their real-world needs will save you time redesigning it later when you find nobody is using it.

## Solution #1: Get to Know Your Users

Know your users before you build anything else. This is the foundational step that most portal projects skip or shortchange -- and it's exactly why so many portals end up unused.

Getting this right means going beyond broad user categories and uncovering the specific needs, contexts and edge cases that real people bring to your portal every day.

## Solution #2: Don't Settle for the Same Old Solutions

### Why Do Healthcare Portals Feel So Outdated?

Most healthcare product teams are forced to use third-party tools that give them no control over the UI or functionality. We've had many conversations with healthcare product teams who desperately want to improve their user experience but tell us there's nothing they can do about the worst parts of that experience.

Chances are that third-party tools are here to stay because there is just too much healthcare data for one organization to manage. But that doesn't mean the tools have to feel like they came from the 90s.

### How Can You Modernize Tools You Don't Fully Control?

Focus on the small opportunities wherever they exist. You may not be able to completely build a new Find a Doctor tool from scratch. But what can you do to take the data from the existing tool and re-imagine how it's presented? Can you challenge the status quo by requiring the developers of your third-party tools to offer better APIs with more UI customization?

By insisting on good tools and design principles wherever possible, you can begin to take back some of the control you've given up to these third-party tools and requirements and advocate for the needs of your users in the process. All it takes is the courage to break free from the functional and familiar to uncover the innovative and delightful.

## Solution #3: Resist the Kitchen Sink Approach

### Why Does Putting Everything in a Portal Make It Worse?

Healthcare portals are designed to be many things for many people. But that doesn't mean they should be all things for all people. It's easy to think of all the features that could be included in a portal, but it's much harder to think of the things that should be left out of it. That's an even harder decision in healthcare, where there are regulations that make it impossible to eliminate certain options or features.

### How Do You Decide What to Prioritize?

Sit down with the people who need to use your portal and understand what is most important to them on a daily basis. Identifying the two or three things that each user type needs the most will help you narrow in on the priorities that need to be available within one click (or less).

Your portal is still likely to have a ton of features, some of which only get used occasionally, but by making the experience as good as possible for the features that are most important you'll help users feel comfortable looking for and finding the other edge case features when they need them.

There will always be edge cases and one-off needs, but if you can knock out the most urgent and important actions and information needs you'll be able to prioritize what is delivered to them in different contexts and on various devices.

## Solution #4: Take the Journey with Your Users

### Why Do Patients Struggle to Navigate Healthcare Portals?

Many healthcare patients don't know where to begin when faced with a new portal they have to use. Many healthcare organizations don't know where to begin in helping them navigate that journey. These two problems feed each other -- and fixing them requires the same thing: mapping the journey out properly.

### What Does Good User Journey Design Look Like in a Patient Portal?

Help users understand the pieces that are confusing. Guide them to the next linear step or related features that they may not know about. Confirm the information they've submitted and clearly communicate to them where they've been, where they are and where they can go next.

In the rush to push out something basically functional and operational, most well-meaning healthcare providers and their product teams overlook the importance of seeing the entire forest for the trees. You need to understand the full journey so you can build the helpful moments that will get your users through that journey. Reduce friction as much as possible and the entire portal will feel new and innovative while remaining compliant with laws and regulations.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why are most patient portals so hard to use?**
Most patient portals are built around the cost-saving priorities of the business rather than the actual needs of the people using them. That means accessibility, different user contexts and real-world workflows often get overlooked in the original build.

**How do I improve a patient portal without replacing the whole thing?**
Focus on small opportunities wherever they exist. Re-imagine how existing data is presented, push third-party tool vendors for better APIs and UI customization options, and apply modern design principles to the pieces you do control.

**What's the biggest mistake healthcare organizations make when designing portals?**
Trying to include everything. The kitchen sink approach buries the features people actually need most. Identifying the two or three priorities for each user type and making those available within one click will do more for adoption than any feature addition.

**How important is user research before building a patient portal?**
It's the most important step you can take. Understanding who your users really are -- including edge cases like users with accessibility needs or staff switching between devices -- will save you from a costly redesign when you discover the portal isn't being used.

**How do you reduce friction in a patient portal?**
Map the full user journey and build in guidance at every step. Confirm submitted information, surface related features proactively, and make it obvious where users are, where they've been and where they can go next. Friction drops when users always know what to do next.
