UX research exists to answer the question your analytics can't: not what your users are doing, but why. Without that, product teams build on assumptions -- and assumptions have a habit of being expensive.

## Why Do UX Research?

Most UX teams live with a background hum of anxiety. They think they know what their users want. They build features and experiences they hope will hit the mark. They might run usability or user acceptance testing to check that what they built works as intended. But in our experience, most organisations don't carve out the time and resources to do the kind of in-depth research that ensures they're solving the right problems in the first place.

After all, what's a product worth if it doesn't help a real person solve a real need in the real world?

This is Part 1 of a two-part series. Here we tackle why to do UX research. Part 2 covers how to do it.

## What Should You Ask Before Starting Any Research?

Before diving into any research project, the most important thing you can do is ask yourself why you're doing it.

- What problem are you trying to solve?
- Who is the research going to help?
- Where, when, and how do you expect to use the insights to improve the product?

You may not have answers to all of these right away. But simply asking them will tell you a lot about whether research is the right investment to make right now.

## What Are the Most Common Reasons Teams Do UX Research?

We're in a unique position at Drawbackwards -- we advise clients at every stage of product development. Some are just getting started and trying to define the problem they want to solve for people. Others have mature products they want to refresh and redefine.

Regardless of where they are in the process, there are three reasons clients typically ask us to help with UX research.

### Reason 1: To Justify Investments the Team Already Believes In

Of all the reasons to do UX research, this might be the weakest -- but it's the most common. Many product teams struggle to secure consistent budget for anything beyond keeping the lights on, yet they already know which areas of the product need the most attention. So research gets used as a tool to "sell" the case for improvements to management and the people holding the purse strings.

There are two problems with this approach.

Going into research loaded with assumptions is never a good idea. You're far more likely to only notice what you're looking for and miss valuable insights hiding in what you didn't expect -- and haven't budgeted for.

You may also uncover more than you bargained for. Open the wrong door and you can find a Pandora's box of problems nobody knew existed, which draws the wrong kind of attention from the C-suite and pulls everyone away from the core problems you set out to solve.

That said, there are times when research genuinely does make sense for justifying additional investment. Just make sure you've narrowly defined the problem you're trying to solve, and go in with an open mind about what you might uncover that you didn't anticipate.

### Reason 2: To Understand the "Why" Behind a Problem

We're all swimming in data. Most products and services have analytics that can track every step of the user or customer journey. What passive analytics don't usually capture is the "why" behind the behaviour. That's where thoughtful, thorough user research earns its keep.

It usually only takes a couple of rounds of building solutions that don't eventually work before a UX or product team learns the value of researching before building.

Exploratory research helps you uncover the deeper motivations of your users and customers, understand their environment, empathise with their real-world problems, and ideate solutions that have a genuine shot at success.

Unfortunately, we've seen too many teams kick the research can down the road and assume they can keep taking stabs at solutions before putting anything in front of actual users.

Problems don't always jump off the page to designers and developers. They need to see the user in action to understand the context in which the solution will exist. With that, they have what they need to make the tweaks that take a product from good and functional to great and delightful.

Don't underestimate the power of simply sitting down with a user to talk about the problem you're trying to solve -- even before you have a solution to offer.

### Reason 3: To Identify Surface-Level UI Problems and Iterate Quickly

There is absolutely nothing wrong with quick usability testing as a pulse check while you're iterating. In fact, we often build light and simple UX research into two-week design sprints for clients. It's a great way to make sure what you're building works as intended and helps you adjust quickly when it doesn't.

But don't let that lull you into feeling like you've got everything covered. You'll still want to make sure your bases are covered with in-depth research that has identified the core problems and characteristics of your users. And you'll want to make sure those quick tests hold up over time.

Keep your finger on the pulse with quick checks, but remember that nothing beats real-world experience -- seeing your product or service in the wild.

## What's the Best Approach to UX Research?

A blended one.

We've worked with clients across a wide range of problems over the years, and we've seen a lot of different motivations for doing research. Some clients are just starting to understand the problems their users face. Others are digging for new understanding.

In each scenario, we look at the underlying problems and make sure we're designing research that uncovers what users actually need -- not just research that scratches an itch for the product or business team to feel better about decisions they've already made.

Mixing your research methods gives you a more holistic view of your product or service and the people who rely on it. Sticking to just one approach leaves you convinced that the trunk is the only part of the elephant that matters.

## FAQ

**Why is UX research important before building a product?**
Without research, teams build on assumptions. UX research replaces guesswork with real understanding of what users need, why they behave the way they do, and what problems are actually worth solving.

**What's the difference between analytics and UX research?**
Analytics tell you what users are doing. UX research tells you why. Both matter, but passive analytics alone won't give you the context or motivation behind user behaviour.

**Is quick usability testing enough, or do you need deeper research?**
Quick usability testing is useful for iterating fast, but it doesn't replace in-depth exploratory research. You need both: regular pulse checks and deeper research that identifies core user problems and validates your direction over time.

**What's wrong with doing UX research just to justify decisions you've already made?**
Going in with your conclusion already written means you'll only look for evidence that confirms it. You'll miss unexpected insights -- and you risk uncovering bigger problems than you were prepared to act on.

**What is a mixed-method approach to UX research?**
A mixed-method approach combines different research types -- such as exploratory interviews, usability testing, and quantitative data -- to build a fuller picture of your users and their needs. Relying on a single method leaves gaps in your understanding.
